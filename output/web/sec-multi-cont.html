    Section 3.1 Multivariate continuous densities

    Though we will not specifically look at special jointly-distributed continuous random variables, we certainly could. Instead here we will focus on the fundamental definitions and properties. This will be handy for example if we someday encountered a situation with a special multivariable probability density (e.g., the bivariate normal).

    Subsection 3.1.1 Multivariate distributions

    See Sec. 3.5 through 3.7.

    Definition 3.1.1. joint probability density.

    If \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous random variables, the function given by \(f(x,y) = P(X=x, Y=y)\) for each point in the \(xy\)-plane \(X\) and \(Y\) is called the joint probability density of \(X\) and \(Y\text{.}\)

    Example 3.1.2. jointly-distributed random variables.

    Consider
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{3}{5}\Big(x\Big(x+y\Big)\Big),\amp \quad 0 \lt x \lt 1; 0 \lt y \lt 2\\ 0,\amp \quad \text{otherwise}\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    and use it to find \(P(0 \lt x \lt 0.5, 1 \lt y \lt 1.5)\text{.}\)
    Hint.
    Set up and evaluate a double integral. Here instead of area under a curve, we are looking for the volume under a surface that has been accumulated over the region of interest.

    Theorem 3.1.4. conditions for a joint probability density.

    A bivariate function can serve as a joint probability density for a pair of continuous random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) if and only if its values, \(f(x, y)\text{,}\) satisfy the conditions
    1. \(f(x, y) \ge 0\) \(-\infty \lt x, y\lt \infty\text{;}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x, y) \,dy\,dx=1\text{.}\)

    Definition 3.1.5. joint distribution function (continuous).

    If \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous random variables, the function given by
    \begin{equation*} F(x, y) = P(X \le x, Y \le y) = \int_{-\infty}^y\int_{-\infty}^x f(s, t) \,ds\,dt\text{ for } -\infty \lt x, y \lt \infty \end{equation*}
    where \(f(s, t)\) is the value of the joint probability density of \(X\) and \(Y\) at \((s, t)\text{,}\) is called the joint density function or joint cumulative density of \(X\) and \(Y\text{.}\)

    Example 3.1.6. joint CDF.

    Consider
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{3}{5}\Big(x\Big(x+y\Big)\Big),\amp \quad 0 \lt x \lt 1; 0 \lt y \lt 2\\ 0,\amp \quad \text{otherwise}\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    and use it to find \(F(x, y) = P(X \lt x, Y \lt y)\text{.}\)
    Hint.
    Set up and evaluate a double integral to an arbitrary point in the plane.
    Solution.
    Here instead of area under a curve, we are looking for the volume under a surface that has been accumulated up to the arbitrary point \((x, y)\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] F(x,y) = \begin{cases} 0 \amp \quad x\le 0\text{ or }y\le 0\\ \dfrac{3x^2y^2}{20} + \dfrac{x^3y}{5} \amp \quad 0 \lt x \lt 1, 0 \lt y \lt 2\\ \dfrac{3y^2}{20}+\dfrac{y}{5} \amp \quad 1\le x, 0 \lt y \lt 2\\ \dfrac{12x^2}{20} +\dfrac{6x^3}{15} \amp \quad 0 \lt x \lt 1, 2\le y\\ 1 \amp \quad 1\le x, 2\le y \end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Notice that for \(x \ge 1\) and \(0 \lt y \lt 2\text{,}\) the result depends only on the \(y\)-value, we are no longer accumulating probability in the \(x\)-direction (lower right region in Figure 3.1.7). Reversing the roles of the variables, the is true for the upper left region where \(0 \lt x \lt 1\) and \(y \ge 2\text{.}\)

    Remark 3.1.8.

    Motivated by \(f(x) = \dfrac{dF}{dx}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f(x, y) = \dfrac{\partial^2}{\partial x \partial y}\Big(F(x, y)\Big) \end{equation*}

    Definition 3.1.9. marginal density.

    If \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous random variables and \(f(x, y)\) is the value of their joint probability density at \((x, y)\text{,}\) the function given by
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = \int f(x, y)\, dy \end{equation*}
    for each \(x\) within the range of \(X\) is called the marginal density of \(X\text{.}\) Correspondingly, the function given by
    \begin{equation*} h(y) = \int f(x, y)\,dx \end{equation*}
    for each \(y\) within the range of \(Y\) is called the marginal density of \(Y\text{.}\)

    Checkpoint 3.1.10. marginal densities.

    Calculate the marginal densities of the joint probability distribution used in Example 3.1.2 and Example 3.1.6

    Definition 3.1.11. conditional density.

    conditional density
    \begin{equation*} f(x|y) = \dfrac{f(x, y)}{h(y)}, h(y)\ne 0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} w(y|x) = \dfrac{f(x, y)}{g(x)}, g(x)\ne 0 \end{equation*}

    Subsection 3.1.2 Product moments

    See section 4.6.

    Definition 3.1.12. product moments about the origin.

    The \(\displaystyle r^\text{th}\) and \(\displaystyle s^\text{th}\) product moment about the origin of the random variables \(X\) and \(Y\text{,}\) denoted by \(\displaystyle \mu_{r,s}\text{,}\) is the expected value of \(\displaystyle X^rY^s\text{;}\) symbolically
    \begin{equation*} \mu_{r,s}'=E[X^rY^s] = \int\int x^r y^s\cdot f(x, y)\,dy\,dx \end{equation*}
    \(\displaystyle r = 0,1,2, \dots\) and \(\displaystyle s = 0,1,2, \dots\) when \(X\) and Y are discrete.
    Special cases of product moments are \(\displaystyle \mu_{1,0}' = E[X^1Y^0] = E[X] = \mu_X\) and \(\displaystyle \mu_{0,1}' = E[X^0Y^1] = E[Y] = \mu_Y\text{.}\)

    Definition 3.1.13. product moments about the mean.

    The \(\displaystyle r^\text{th}\) and \(\displaystyle s^\text{th}\) product moment about the mean of the random variables \(X\) and \(Y\text{,}\) denoted by \(\displaystyle \mu_{r,s}'\text{,}\) is the expected value of \(\displaystyle (X-\mu_X)^r(Y-\mu_Y)^s\text{;}\) symbolically
    \begin{equation*} \mu_{r,s}=E[(X-\mu_X)^r(Y-\mu_Y)^s] = \int\int (x-\mu_X)^r (y-\mu_Y)^s\cdot f(x, y)\,dy\,dx \end{equation*}
    \(\displaystyle r = 0,1,2, \dots\) and \(\displaystyle s = 0,1,2, \dots\) when \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous.
    Now would be a good time to review Definition 4.1.19, Theorem 4.1.20, Theorem 4.1.21, Remark 4.1.22, and Theorem 4.1.23 all of which apply here as well.
    Independence means covariance is zero, but covariances of zero does not mean independence.

    Exercises 3.1.3 Exercises

    1. Problem 4.41.

    If \(X\) and \(Y\) have the joint probability distribution \(f(x, y) = \dfrac{1}{4}\) for \((-3, -5)\text{,}\) \((-1, -1)\text{,}\) \((1, 1)\text{,}\) \((3, 5)\text{,}\) find \(\operatorname{cov}(X, Y)\text{.}\)

    2. Problem 4.45.

    If \(X\) and \(Y\) have the joint probability distribution \(f(-1, 0) = 0\text{,}\) \(f(-1, 1) = \dfrac{1}{4}\text{,}\) \(f(0, 0) = \dfrac{1}{6}\text{,}\) \(f(1, 0) = \dfrac{1}{12}\text{,}\) \(f(1, 1) = \dfrac{1}{2}\) show that
    1. \(\operatorname{cov}(X, Y) = 0\text{;}\)
    2. the two random variables are not independent.

    Subsection 3.1.4 Conditional expectation

    See section 4.8.

    Definition 3.1.14. conditional expectation.

    If \(X\) is a discrete random variable and \(\displaystyle f(x|y)\) is the value of the conditional probability distribution of \(X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) at \(X\text{,}\) the conditional expectation of \(\displaystyle u(X)\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) is
    \begin{equation*} E[u(X)|y] = \int u(x)\cdot f(x|y)\,dx \end{equation*}
    and the conditional expectation of \(\displaystyle v(Y)\) given \(\displaystyle X = x\) is
    \begin{equation*} E[v(Y)|x] = \int v(y)\cdot w(y|x)\,dy \end{equation*}

    Definition 3.1.15. conditional mean.

    If \(X\) is a discrete random variable and \(\displaystyle f(x|y)\) is the value of the conditional probability distribution of \(X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) at \(X\text{,}\) the conditional mean of \(\displaystyle u(X) = X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) is
    \begin{equation*} \mu_{X|y} = E[X|y] = \int x\cdot f(x|y)\,dx \end{equation*}
    and the conditional mean of \(\displaystyle v(Y) = Y\) given \(\displaystyle X = x\) is
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \mu_{Y|x} = E[Y|x] = \int y\cdot w(y|x)\,dy \end{equation*}

    Definition 3.1.16. conditional variance.

    If \(X\) is a discrete random variable and \(\displaystyle f(x|y)\) is the value of the conditional probability distribution of \(X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) at \(X\text{,}\) the conditional variance of \(X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) is
    \begin{equation*} \sigma^2_{X|y} = E[(X-\mu_{X|y})^2|y] = E[X^2]-\mu^2_{X|y} \end{equation*}
    and the conditional expectation of \(Y\) given \(\displaystyle X = x\) is
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle\sigma^2_{Y|x} = E[(Y-\mu_{Y|x})^2|y] = E[Y^2]-\mu^2_{Y|x} \end{equation*}
